MODULE 1: CLAIM SUBSTANTIATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RULE 1.1 — Every factual claim must be substantiable.
Written rule: All descriptions, claims and comparisons must be capable of
substantiation.

How ASCI applies it in practice:
- Any percentage, ratio, or number ("2x faster", "47% reduction", "220% more
  hydration") is treated as a factual claim requiring proof — no exceptions.
- "#1 in India" or "India's best" requires citing the specific metric, research
  body, geography, and date. "Best" alone without context is not puffery —
  ASCI treats it as a claim.
- Claims about an ingredient's performance do NOT cover claims about the
  finished product. "Our formula contains X which is proven to reduce Y" only
  passes if the proof is on the finished product, not isolated ingredient data.
- "Up to X%" is not a free pass. If the headline implies X% is typical but
  fine print says "up to", that gap is a violation of 1.4 (misleading by
  omission), not saved by the disclaimer.

Flag: any number, ranking, superlative, or outcome claim without an
immediately visible, specific source.

---

RULE 1.2 — Research citations must include source AND date.
Written rule: Claims based on independent research must indicate source and date.

How ASCI applies it in practice:
- "Clinically proven", "dermatologist tested", "scientifically proven",
  "studies show", "lab tested", "doctor recommended" — ALL require:
  institution name + date + sample size.
- "Dermatologist recommended" with no number is a violation.
  "9 out of 10 dermatologists" requires sample size and methodology.
- Certifications (ISO, ECOCERT, etc.) are acceptable evidence IF the
  certifying body is named and the certification is current.
- Internal studies by the brand itself are not considered independent research.

---

RULE 1.4 — No misleading by omission or implication.
Written rule: Advertisements cannot mislead the consumer by means of
implications or omissions.

How ASCI applies it in practice:
- A disclaimer that contradicts or significantly qualifies the headline claim
  is itself evidence of a violation — the headline is the violation, not fixed
  by the disclaimer.
- Selective data: showing only the best-performing user segment as if it
  represents all users.
- Before/after visuals where lighting, angle, filters, or post-processing
  differ between shots — treated as misleading by omission of those factors.
- "Results may vary" does not save a claim that shows extreme or atypical
  results as representative.

---

RULE 1.5 — No exploiting consumer inexperience or trust.

Sub-rule 1.5.1: "Free" must mean free.
Flag: any asterisk, condition, or hidden cost attached to a "free" offer.
Flag: "free gift with purchase" where the gift inflates the product price.

Sub-rule 1.5.3: Conditional claims.
Flag: "up to X%" when presented as if X% is the standard outcome.
Flag: "earn up to Rs.50,000/month" in edtech/course ads without context.

Sub-rule 1.5.4: Health/body claims.
Flag: any claim implying typical or guaranteed results for weight, skin, hair,
strength, energy, or other physical attributes without specific evidence.
Flag: transformation imagery (before/after bodies, skin, hair) without
disclaimer that results vary.

Sub-rule 1.5.5: Investment/financial claims.
Flag: any promise of security, returns, or profit without explicit risk
disclosure in equal prominence.
Flag: "your money is safe", "guaranteed X% returns", "risk-free investment".

---

RULE 1.6 — Humor/hyperbole exception.
A claim is ONLY protected as obvious hyperbole if a reasonable average consumer
would immediately recognize it as impossible or absurd.
"Our pizza is so good it'll change your life" = protected hyperbole.
"Our supplement boosts immunity by 300%" = factual-sounding claim, not hyperbole.

MODULE 2: HARMFUL CONTENT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RULE 3.1.2 — No discrimination or harmful stereotyping.
Written rule: No demeaning of individuals by race, caste, color, religion,
gender, body shape, age, sexual orientation, or physical/mental conditions.

How ASCI applies it in practice:
- Linking lighter skin to success, attractiveness, marriage prospects,
  employment, or confidence = violation. This applies even when subtle
  (e.g., the "after" person in a fairness ad gets the job or the date).
- Associating body weight/shape with laziness, undesirability, or failure.
- Showing only one gender doing domestic tasks while the other relaxes.
- "Chemical-free is better for your family" implies chemical = harmful,
  which ASCI treats as misleading framing, not just a green claim issue.

---

RULE 3.2 — Child safety. AUTO-FAIL if:
- Children shown near fire, electrical appliances, sharp objects, explosives.
- Children featured in tobacco, alcohol, gambling, or adult health product ads.
- Ad encourages children to speak to strangers or enter unsafe locations.
- Sports/entertainment celebrities used to promote health-warning products
  where children are likely to see the ad.

---

RULE 3.3 — Dangerous practices.
Flag any ad showing or implying unsafe behavior (speeding, stunts, reckless
acts) without a visible cautionary message.

MODULE 3: PROHIBITED CATEGORIES — AUTO-FAIL + ESCALATION FLAG
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

These are not ASCI code violations — they are illegal under Indian law.
Ads in these categories are reported directly to government regulators.
Mark as AUTO-FAIL with escalation flag, regardless of how the ad is framed.

- Offshore or illegal betting/gambling platforms (even if disguised as
  "fantasy sports", "skill games", or promoted through influencer bios,
  brand tickers, or community pages).
- Products claiming to cure, treat, or prevent diseases listed under the
  Drugs & Magic Remedies Act 1954 (DMR Act). Includes: cancer, diabetes,
  arthritis, hair loss cures, sexual dysfunction, weight loss "guaranteed".
- Direct promotion of alcohol beverages on social media.
- Unauthorised forex trading apps (not on RBI's approved list).
- Tobacco products.
- Crypto ads without mandatory disclaimer.

How ASCI applies it in practice (from 2024-25 and 2025-26 data):
- Betting ads disguised as celebrity endorsements, fan pages, or "investment
  tips" are still treated as betting ads.
- Ayurvedic/herbal products claiming to cure medical conditions fall under
  DMR Act — "natural" does not exempt them.
- Alcohol brand extensions (music CDs, water, etc.) with strong visual/name
  cues to the alcohol brand are treated as surrogate advertising.

MODULE 4: DISCLOSURE RULES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RULE I-1.1 — Disclosure required for any material connection.
Material connection includes: payment, free products (even unsolicited),
discounts, gifted travel/stays/experiences, barter deals, contra deals,
any family or employment relationship with the brand.

How ASCI applies it in practice:
- "I bought this myself" is only valid if the influencer can produce proof
  of purchase AND confirm no material connection with the brand.
- Receiving even one free product creates a material connection for ALL
  subsequent posts about that brand until the connection ends.
- Agency briefing an influencer = material connection, even if the brief
  says "share your honest opinion."

---

RULE I-1.2 — Disclosure must be upfront and unmissable.

FAIL conditions (directly from real violation patterns — 56.8% no disclosure,
43.2% wrong placement in ASCI's 2024 dipstick study):
- Disclosure only in hashtags (#ad buried among 20 other hashtags).
- Disclosure in bio or "About Me" only.
- Disclosure requires clicking "more" / "see more" to see.
- Disclosure appears only at end of video or caption.
- Disclosure in font visibly smaller than surrounding text.
- Disclosure color blends into background.

Video timing rules (strictly enforced):
- 15 seconds or less: disclosure visible for minimum 3 continuous seconds.
- 15 seconds to 2 minutes: visible for at least 1/3 of total video duration.
- 2 minutes or longer: visible throughout the entire segment where brand/product
  is mentioned or shown.
- Live streams: announced verbally at start AND end.
- Stories/Snapchat (no text): label superimposed on the visual, clearly readable.

Audio-only (podcasts, audio ads):
- Disclosure announced at the start AND end AND before/after every break.

---

RULE I-1.3 — Only these disclosure labels are valid:
"Advertisement", "Ad", "Sponsored", "Collaboration", "Partnership",
"Employee", "Free Gift", "Affiliate", "Paid Partnership" (Instagram native
tag), "Includes Paid Promotion" (YouTube native tag).

Invalid (from actual violation cases):
- "Collab" alone (not on the approved list).
- "Gifted" alone.
- "In association with" (too vague).
- "Thanks [brand] for sending this" (not a disclosure label).
- Emoji-only disclosure.
- "Check out my fave brand" with no label.
- Disclosure only in a language different from the ad content.

Language rule: disclosure must be in English OR the same language as the
ad content. Bilingual ad = disclosure required in both languages.

---

RULE I-1.4 — Virtual influencers must disclose non-human status.
CGI or AI-generated presenters must explicitly state they are not real humans.
This disclosure must be upfront, not in the fine print.

---

RULE I-1.5 — Both brand AND influencer are responsible.
If the brand can be identified, both are liable for accuracy of all claims
and presence of disclosure. Flag the claim violation AND note that both
parties are liable.

---

RULE I-Addendum2 — Qualified advice (LEGALLY BACKED BY CCPA 2023):
This is one of the highest-risk rules for brands and influencers currently.

BFSI category (investments, insurance, financial planning):
Technical advice requires: SEBI registration (investments) OR IRDAI license /
CA / CS qualification (other finance).
Credentials must appear: superimposed on video upfront, OR opening remark in
audio, OR stated before the content in text posts.

Health & nutrition category:
Technical advice requires: medical degree, nursing certification,
nutritionist/dietician/physiotherapist/psychologist qualification.
Credentials must appear in same format as above.

What counts as "technical advice" (flag these):
- Specific supplement dosage recommendations.
- "This fixes your thyroid / PCOD / gut issues."
- "Invest in X for Y% returns."
- "This plan is better than [other financial product]."
- Specific diet protocols for medical conditions.

What counts as "generic promotion" (allowed without credentials):
- "Get your annual health checkup."
- "This protein shake is part of my morning routine."
- "Health insurance gives you peace of mind."
- "This meal service makes cooking easier."

MODULE 5: SECTOR-SPECIFIC RULES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Detect the primary sector from ad content and apply the relevant ruleset.
Multiple sectors may apply simultaneously.

--- PERSONAL CARE (skincare, haircare, cosmetics) ---
Highest volume misleading ads category. Apply extra scrutiny.

- Before/after: flag if lighting, angle, filters, makeup, or post-processing
  differ between shots. Flag if no disclaimer "results may vary."
- Outcome stats ("reduces wrinkles by 47%", "90% saw improvement"): must be
  from the finished product in a real-world trial. Ingredient-level lab data
  does not count.
- Skin lightening/fairness: flag any association of skin tone with social,
  romantic, or professional outcomes — even subtle visual storytelling.
- "Dermatologist recommended/approved/tested": flag without number + methodology.
- "X% more hydration/strength/growth": flag without product-level trial data.
- 53% of personal care violations in 2025 involved influencer non-disclosure
  — check disclosure compliance with double scrutiny for this sector.

--- HEALTHCARE / PHARMA / SUPPLEMENTS / AYURVEDA ---
81.6% of healthcare violations were DMR Act violations — check this first.

- Any claim to cure, treat, prevent, or remedy a named medical condition =
  check against DMR Act. If it names a condition → AUTO-FAIL.
- "Boosts immunity", "detoxifies", "cleanses the body", "anti-inflammatory":
  flag as unsubstantiated unless specific mechanism + peer-reviewed evidence cited.
- "Clinically tested/proven": requires testing body name, date, sample size,
  and that testing was on the finished product.
- "No side effects", "completely safe", "100% safe": flag as unsubstantiated
  absolute claims.
- Ayurvedic/herbal products are NOT exempt from these rules.

--- FOOD & BEVERAGES ---
Health & nutrition subclaims are 61% of F&B violations.

- "Healthy", "nutritious", "natural", "wholesome": flag unless backed by
  specific nutritional data or FSSAI approval.
- Transformation claims from consuming the product: flag unless clinical
  evidence cited.
- "Meal replacement": only if FSSAI-approved as a meal replacement product.
- Alcohol: any direct promotion on any social media platform = AUTO-FAIL.
- "No added sugar" / "low fat" / "zero calories": must comply with FSSAI
  definition thresholds.

--- EDUCATION / EDTECH ---
- "100% placement", "guaranteed job", "guaranteed salary of X" = AUTO-FAIL.
- All placement statistics must be from the most recent completed academic year.
- Recognition/affiliation claims must name the specific authority with current validity.
- Mandatory disclaimer for any placement/outcome claim:
  "Past record is no guarantee of future prospects."
- Testimonials only from students who completed the full program.
- "Ranked #1 edtech platform": must name ranking body, metric, date, geography.

--- REAL ESTATE ---
- Maharashtra ads: MahaRERA project registration number required.
  QR code with project details required. Absence = flag for MahaRERA reporting.
- "Guaranteed rental returns", "assured returns of X%": flag as investment
  claim requiring risk disclosure.
- Price appreciation projections without evidence = Rule 1.1 violation.
- "RERA approved" without registration number = insufficient.

--- CRYPTO / VIRTUAL DIGITAL ASSETS ---
- Mandatory disclaimer required verbatim:
  "Crypto products and NFTs are unregulated and can be highly risky.
   There may be no regulatory recourse for any loss from such transactions."
  Flag if absent, truncated, or in small/unreadable text.
- Banned words in crypto ads: "currency", "securities", "custodian".
- "Guaranteed profit", "assured returns", "safe crypto investment" = AUTO-FAIL.
- No minors depicted.
- Influencers promoting crypto without SEBI registration = Addendum 2 violation.

--- FINANCIAL PRODUCTS / INVESTMENTS ---
- Any return projection must be accompanied by risk disclosure in equal prominence.
- "Safe", "guaranteed", "risk-free", "assured returns" = AUTO-FAIL.
- Insurance: must not overstate coverage or understate exclusions.

--- GREEN / ENVIRONMENTAL CLAIMS ---
100% of green claim ads required modification in ASCI's 2024-25 review.

Flag ALL of these without substantiation:
"Eco-friendly", "sustainable", "biodegradable", "plastic-free", "chemical-free",
"100% natural", "environmentally safe", "green", "carbon neutral",
"carbon negative", "zero waste", "non-toxic", "clean", "pure."

- Evidence must be based on full product lifecycle unless scope is explicitly stated.
- Certifying body must be named for any certification claim or badge.
- "Chemical-free" = scientifically impossible. Flag as absolute misleading claim.
- "Natural" alone = insufficient. Must specify what is natural and evidence it.

--- FASHION & LIFESTYLE ---
- Body image: flag any content implying a specific body type is required
  for happiness, success, or desirability.
- Gender stereotyping: flag role assignments by gender.
- "Sustainable fashion", "ethical sourcing", "organic fabric": apply green
  claims rules above.

--- AWARDS & RANKINGS ---
- Must state: awarding organization full name + month + year of award.
- Cannot claim a ranking across geographies unless independently verified.
- "#1 in [category]": must name the ranking methodology and body.

--- AUTOMOTIVE ---
- Stunts, high-speed driving, off-road scenes: require "Professional driver
  on closed course. Do not attempt." disclaimer.
- Fuel efficiency claims: must reflect standard test conditions and state them.

MODULE 6: DISCLAIMER STANDARDS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Flag disclaimers that:
- Appear for less than 4 seconds on screen per line.
- Are in font smaller than readable size relative to the ad.
  (TV minimum: 14px SD / 26px HD / 57px 4K, lowercase letter height)
  (Print minimum: 7pt for ads 50cc or less, 9pt for 100cc or less, 10pt for over 100cc)
- Contradict or significantly qualify the headline claim.
- Use complex legal language an average consumer cannot understand.
- Are placed where unlikely to be seen (extreme bottom, white-on-white).

MODULE 7: COMPARATIVE ADVERTISING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Comparative ads are allowed ONLY IF all of these are true:
- The compared aspects are clearly and specifically identified.
- The comparison is factual and independently substantiable.
- It does not mislead about the competitor's product.
- It does not denigrate or mock the competitor.

Flag if:
- Competitor is named/shown but comparison methodology is not stated.
- "Better than leading brands" without naming which brands and on what metric.
- Competitor depicted negatively beyond the specific factual comparison.

MODULE 8: ONLINE DECEPTIVE PRACTICES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Flag:
- Drip pricing: taxes, fees, or charges not shown in headline price.
- False urgency: "Only 2 left!" or countdown timers that reset.
- Bait and switch: ad shows premium product, landing page shows different.
- Fake social proof: "10,000 customers bought this today" without basis.
- Disguised ads that look like editorial content or user reviews.
